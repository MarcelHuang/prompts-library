# Start From Side Hustle

Starting a business on the side while keeping your full-time job is one of the most well-documented paths to success in David's highlights—and there's a deep well of wisdom here, from Phil Knight selling shoes for seven years while teaching, to Larry Ellison building Oracle as a side hustle, to Joseph Kennedy running multiple side companies while employed at a brokerage. Here are prompts tailored specifically to your situation:

## Prompts on the Transition (Job → Full-Time Founder)

- **"How did founders know when it was time to quit their job and go full-time?"** This unlocks the incredibly relatable internal struggle captured in [The Republic of Tea letters](https://www.foundersnotes.com/transcripts/2089/?location=01:00:04), where Bill writes about being "pulled" between the income from his day job and his unproven dream—admitting "it's tough when your head and heart have to be at odds." It also surfaces Phil Knight finally quitting his teaching job right before his 31st birthday in [Phil Knight (Nike)](https://www.foundersnotes.com/transcripts/2094/?location=01:06:10).

- **"How long did successful founders work on their business before going full-time?"** Phil Knight's story in [Nike](https://www.foundersnotes.com/transcripts/2094/?location=01:06:10) is the gold standard—seven years of every free minute before he could justify a salary. David's reflection there is striking: "how many people would have given up before that?"

## Prompts on Running a Side Business Well

- **"What does David's collection teach about juggling a job and a side business at the same time?"** This brings up Joseph Kennedy, who "constantly had side businesses, side hustles going on" while employed—running two real estate companies AND a full-time brokerage job in [The Remarkable Life of Joseph P. Kennedy](https://www.foundersnotes.com/transcripts/2129/?location=00:21:58).

- **"How did Andrew Carnegie use his day job to fund and discover side investments?"** A brilliant case study in [Andrew Carnegie and Henry Clay Frick](https://www.foundersnotes.com/transcripts/2030/?location=00:12:10)—Carnegie made his first fortune in oil investments while still holding a full-time railroad job, using his position to access opportunities others couldn't see.

- **"How did Larry Ellison start Oracle as a side job?"** This surfaces the fascinating origin in [Larry Ellison and Oracle](https://www.foundersnotes.com/transcripts/2163/?location=00:18:10)—one of the world's largest companies began as a side hustle while he was employed at Omex.

## Prompts on Focus vs. Moonlighting

- **"Is it better to focus on one thing or keep multiple irons in the fire?"** This reveals a productive tension in David's highlights—Ray Kroc's lesson in [Grinding It Out](https://www.foundersnotes.com/bookreview/1549/?highlight=53435) that he was "trying to work 2 jobs and failing at both" until he focused, versus Joseph Kennedy's success keeping many side businesses running. Worth wrestling with.

- **"What's the smallest way I can start while still employed?"** This taps Steve Jobs' advice in [In the Company of Giants](https://www.foundersnotes.com/bookreview/1448/?highlight=49497) where one founder began "as a part-time job in the second bedroom of my apartment" with just $2,000—and Adi Dassler starting Adidas in his mother's washroom in [Adi Dassler (Adidas)](https://www.foundersnotes.com/transcripts/2161/?location=01:12:08).

## Prompts on Mindset While You Have a Safety Net

- **"How do I balance the security of my paycheck with the risk of building something new?"** This pulls Estée Lauder's argument in [Estée Lauder](https://www.foundersnotes.com/transcripts/2352/?location=00:03:09) that "risk taking is the cornerstone of empires"—powerful to weigh against the comfort of a steady income.

- **"How did young founders like Henry Frick structure brutally efficient schedules?"** This surfaces Frick's ambitious daily routine in [Henry Clay Frick](https://www.foundersnotes.com/transcripts/2124/?location=00:26:08)—working a full day, then night school until 9:30—a template for anyone trying to build something after their 9-to-5.

- **"Why might starting on the side actually be an advantage, not a limitation?"** This connects Jack Ma working part-time on his first company in [Alibaba](https://www.foundersnotes.com/transcripts/2252/?location=00:38:02) with the research David cites there—debunking the wunderkind myth, noting a 46-year-old is 3x more likely to found a successful business than a 25-year-old.

# Fundamentals of Business

Learning the fundamentals of business is a recurring obsession across David's highlights—and the beautiful thread running through all of them is that the fundamentals are simple, unchanging, and the same masters who dominate any field (from Kobe to Rockefeller) are the ones who drill the basics relentlessly. Here are prompts designed to extract that core wisdom:

## Prompts on What the Fundamentals Actually Are

- **"What are the simplest, most universal rules of business across David's highlights?"** This surfaces J. Paul Getty's ten fundamental rules in [How to Be Rich](https://www.foundersnotes.com/bookreview/2334/?highlight=86745) and Don Valentine's brutally simple framework quoted in [Kobe Bryant (Mamba Mentality)](https://www.foundersnotes.com/transcripts/2059/?location=00:28:03): "There are two things in business that matter... high gross margins and cash flow."

- **"What did Henry Ford believe were the fundamental principles of running a business?"** This unlocks Ford's four principles in [My Life and Work](https://www.foundersnotes.com/transcripts/2198/?location=00:50:06)—including his radical idea that service must come before profit, and that "profit must be the result of service."

- **"What's the most important asset in running a business according to David's highlights?"** This pulls Buffett's answer in ["All I Want to Know Is Where I'm Going to Die"](https://www.foundersnotes.com/bookreview/1386/?highlight=45985): in business, "it's passion"—and Helzberg's distilled truth that "Business is people."

## Prompts on Why Mastering Basics Beats Getting Fancy

- **"Why do the greatest founders insist on mastering fundamentals over advanced tactics?"** This is one of the richest themes in David's collection—the Kobe/Michael Jordan phone call appears again and again, like in [A Few Lessons From Warren Buffett](https://www.foundersnotes.com/transcripts/2301/?location=00:50:15), where Buffett echoes that a manager "must handle the basics well and not get diverted."

- **"What does Naval Ravikant say about there being no skill called 'business'?"** This surfaces a provocative idea in [Naval Ravikant (A Guide to Wealth and Happiness)](https://www.foundersnotes.com/transcripts/2326/?location=00:19:00): avoid business magazines and classes, and instead master foundational skills like microeconomics, psychology, and persuasion.

## Prompts on Picking the Right Market (A Core Fundamental)

- **"Why does Warren Buffett say the business you choose matters more than how brilliant you are?"** This pulls one of the most repeated lessons in David's highlights, from [The Essays of Warren Buffett](https://www.foundersnotes.com/transcripts/2196/?location=00:34:09): "eroding fundamentals will overwhelm managerial brilliance"—and his wise friend's quip in [Berkshire Hathaway Letters](https://www.foundersnotes.com/bookreview/1643/?highlight=62312): "If you want to get a reputation as a good businessman, be sure to get into a good business."

- **"What's the difference between a 'franchise' and just a 'business'?"** This unlocks Buffett's excellent breakdown in [Warren Buffett's Shareholder Letters](https://www.foundersnotes.com/transcripts/2218/?location=01:54:45)—a great mental model for understanding pricing power, moats, and which businesses can survive bad management.

## Prompts on the Best Way to Actually Learn

- **"What's the single best exercise for truly understanding how a business works?"** This surfaces Li Lu's powerful "inherited business" thought experiment in [Li Lu in His Own Words](https://www.foundersnotes.com/bookreview/2353/?highlight=87281): imagine you inherited 100% of one business—what would you do? Understand it inside and out.

- **"Why does David believe reading biographies teaches business fundamentals better than business school?"** This connects Charlie Munger's argument in [Tao of Charlie Munger](https://www.foundersnotes.com/bookreview/1642/?highlight=62273) that "one is actually better off reading a hundred business biographies than a hundred books on investing" with Elon Musk learning from "historical context" rather than business books.

- **"How do I learn the fundamentals of advertising and customer acquisition?"** This pulls from Claude Hopkins' [Scientific Advertising](https://www.foundersnotes.com/transcripts/2219/?location=00:08:04)—the foundational principles of CAC and running experiments that made countless entrepreneurs wealthy.

## Prompts on the Mindset Fundamentals

- **"What's Warren Buffett's formula for the mindset to have when running a business?"** This surfaces his beloved three-part rule in [The Essays of Warren Buffett](https://www.foundersnotes.com/bookreview/1430/?highlight=48416): run it as if you own 100%, it's your family's only asset, and you can't sell it for a century.

- **"Why is simplicity the most important fundamental of all?"** This taps Munger's wisdom via [Robert Kierlin: Founder of Fastenal](https://www.foundersnotes.com/transcripts/2351/?location=00:17:58)—that "the most elusive of all human goals is keeping things simple and remembering what you set out to do."

---

If I had to point you to the single most valuable starting place: ask me **"Why do the greatest founders insist on mastering fundamentals over advanced tactics?"** The Kobe/Jordan story threaded through David's highlights is the perfect entry point—it reframes business learning as a discipline of relentlessly drilling the basics, exactly the way Michael Jordan practiced a simple chest pass before every game. # Business Math
Business math, across David's highlights, is refreshingly un-academic—the masters didn't run complex models, they obsessed over a handful of numbers (margins, cash flow, return on investment) and *physically* internalized them by hand. Here are prompts designed to extract that practical numeracy:

## Prompts on the Numbers That Actually Matter

- **"What are the only two numbers Don Valentine says matter in business?"** This unlocks the brutally simple framework that high gross margins and cash flow are what count—a theme that echoes through Charlie Munger's two-kinds-of-businesses test in [Tao of Charlie Munger](https://www.foundersnotes.com/bookreview/1382/?highlight=45640): one earns 12% and you can take the cash out, the other earns 12% but must reinvest it all and "there's never any cash."

- **"Why is cash flow more important than profit, according to David's highlights?"** This surfaces J. Paul Getty's hard rule in [How to Get Rich](https://www.foundersnotes.com/bookreview/1518/?highlight=52444) that "cash flow is something any entrepreneur must fully comprehend from the get-go," and Michael Dell's near-death lesson in [Direct From Dell](https://www.foundersnotes.com/bookreview/2819/?highlight=88647)—watching only the P&L "speedometer" while the company was secretly "running out of gas," reordering his priorities to "liquidity, profitability, and growth—in that order."

- **"Why does Sloan say return on investment supersedes other business measures?"** This pulls Alfred Sloan's framework in [My Years With General Motors](https://www.foundersnotes.com/bookreview/1608/?highlight=56818)—that profit on sales and market penetration matter, but never supersede return on investment.

## Prompts on Margins and Pricing Power

- **"Why did the greatest founders obsess over profit margins?"** This brings up Henry Singleton's incentive system at Teledyne in [Henry Singleton (The Outsiders)](https://www.foundersnotes.com/transcripts/2193/?location=00:26:53)—where high margins were the "report card to HQ"—alongside the staggering software margins (83%!) that kept Microsoft alive while NeXT collapsed, documented in [Steve Jobs & the NeXT Big Thing](https://www.foundersnotes.com/bookreview/1638/?highlight=61957).

- **"Why does Carnegie say to watch costs and let profits take care of themselves?"** This is one of the richest math lessons in the collection, from [Andrew Carnegie and Henry Clay Frick](https://www.foundersnotes.com/transcripts/2306/?location=00:34:12): "Cut the prices, scoop the market, watch the costs and the profits will take care of themselves"—because revenue and profits are cyclical, but cost savings are permanent.

- **"How small can cost savings be and still matter at scale?"** This surfaces the brilliant Costco shopping-bag math in [Charlie Munger (the Tao of Charlie Munger)](https://www.foundersnotes.com/transcripts/2235/?location=00:42:19)—how $0.10 per bag across 150 million checkouts equals ~$45 million a year, teaching you to think multiplicatively about tiny variables.

## Prompts on Understanding Unit Economics

- **"How did Jeff Bezos use unit economics to stay calm while his stock crashed?"** This pulls the unforgettable scene in [Invent and Wander](https://www.foundersnotes.com/bookreview/1575/?highlight=54703)—Bezos watching the stock fall from 113 to 6 while internal metrics (profit per unit, fixed-cost leverage) kept improving, knowing exactly the volume needed to cover fixed costs.

- **"Why is it dangerous to optimize the bottom line over the top line?"** This connects Kinko's founder's "corporate dyslexia" passport-photo story in [Copy This!](https://www.foundersnotes.com/bookreview/1483/?highlight=51237)—where budget people killed a Yellow Pages ad as an "expense" without seeing it drove a $13 product at $1.75 cost—with Steve Jobs' rule that managing the top line lets the bottom line follow, quoted in [Yvon Chouinard (Patagonia)](https://www.foundersnotes.com/transcripts/2134/?location=00:54:03).

## Prompts on the Math of Leverage and Survival

- **"Why does Buffett say any series of numbers multiplied by a single zero equals zero?"** This is the single most important piece of business math in David's highlights, appearing in both [The Essays of Warren Buffett](https://www.foundersnotes.com/bookreview/1430/?highlight=48448) and [his shareholder letters](https://www.foundersnotes.com/transcripts/2196/?location=01:08:05)—the equation that explains why leverage is "like oxygen" and why survival math beats return math.

- **"Why does J. Paul Getty advise keeping a fortress of cash and using debt sparingly?"** This surfaces his philosophy in [As I See It](https://www.foundersnotes.com/bookreview/2334/?highlight=86736)—that "more businesses die from indigestion than starvation."

## Prompts on the Long-Term Math of Compounding

- **"Why does 75% of a business's value come from profits 10+ years out?"** This unlocks a genuinely mind-bending calculation in [A Few Lessons From Warren Buffett](https://www.foundersnotes.com/transcripts/2301/?location=00:26:07)—reframing how you value a moat that widens over decades, with the Amazon/Apple "they now make in 8 hours what they made in a year" illustration.

- **"Why does Charlie Munger call EBITDA 'b******* earnings'?"** This pulls his sharp lesson in [Charlie Munger (the Tao of Charlie Munger)](https://www.foundersnotes.com/transcripts/2235/?location=00:28:03)—a crucial filter for not fooling yourself with made-up metrics that ignore real capital costs.

## Prompts on How to Actually Internalize the Numbers

- **"Why did Sam Walton write every store's P&L by hand?"** This is my favorite practical-math story in the collection, in [Sam Walton: Made In America](https://www.foundersnotes.com/transcripts/2287/?location=00:54:02)—a 15-column ledger he filled in by pen each month because writing the numbers himself "helped me remember them better," carrying it in his pocket to every store.

- **"Why does Oprah sign every check?"** This surfaces a powerful discipline in [Oprah](https://www.foundersnotes.com/bookreview/1352/?highlight=43565)—with David's note that Henry Singleton called signing every check "a form of discipline" and "an education."

## A Crucial Counterbalance

- **"What did Charlie Munger and John Bogle warn about over-relying on numbers?"** Don't skip this one. It surfaces Munger's warning in [Poor Charlie's Almanack](https://www.foundersnotes.com/bookreview/1355/?highlight=43717) about overweighting "the stuff that can be numbered," and Bogle's beautiful line in [Enough](https://www.foundersnotes.com/transcripts/2139/?location=00:26:04): "not everything that counts can be counted and not everything that can be counted counts." Essential perspective so you don't confuse the map with the territory.

If you want the single best starting point: ask me **"Why does Sam Walton write every store's P&L by hand?"** It pairs perfectly with Oprah signing every check—together they teach you that learning business math isn't about formulas, it's about getting your hands dirty in your own numbers until you can feel them. # Right Market

Picking the right market is, without exaggeration, one of the most repeated and most emphasized lessons across David's entire collection—it threads through Warren Buffett, Marc Andreessen, Peter Thiel, and Jay-Z alike. The core idea, as Andreessen puts it, is that "markets that don't exist don't care how smart you are." Here are prompts designed to extract every angle of this lesson:

## Prompts on Why the Market Matters More Than Anything

- **"Why does Warren Buffett say the business 'boat' matters more than how effectively you row?"** This is the foundational lesson, appearing in [The Essays of Warren Buffett](https://www.foundersnotes.com/bookreview/1430/?highlight=48438) and again in [his shareholder letters](https://www.foundersnotes.com/transcripts/2218/?location=01:26:11): "When a management with a reputation for brilliance tackles a business with a reputation for poor fundamental economics, it is the reputation of the business that remains intact."

- **"What does Marc Andreessen say is the single most important factor in startup success—team, product, or market?"** This unlocks his definitive essay in [Marc Andreessen's Blog Archive](https://www.foundersnotes.com/transcripts/2307/?location=00:16:01), built around Rachleff's Law: "When a great team meets a lousy market, the market wins... Market matters most."

- **"How does Jay-Z's lyric 'ain't nothing wrong with the aim, just gotta change the target' relate to picking a market?"** A surprising and brilliant connection in [Jay Z: Decoded](https://www.foundersnotes.com/transcripts/2226/?location=00:25:59)—David ties street-level hustler wisdom directly to Buffett and Andreessen's market-selection principle.

## Prompts on Starting Small and Dominating a Niche

- **"What does Peter Thiel say is the perfect target market for a startup?"** This surfaces the powerful idea in [Peter Thiel](https://www.foundersnotes.com/transcripts/2237/?location=00:30:00): "a small group of particular people concentrated together and served by few or no competitors"—plus how Amazon deliberately started with books before dominating all of retail.

- **"Why does sequencing markets correctly matter, and why do MBA types miss it?"** This pulls from [Zero to One](https://www.foundersnotes.com/bookreview/1570/?highlight=58522)—"the initial markets are so small that they often don't even appear to be business opportunities at all"—and the discipline it takes to expand gradually.

## Prompts on Finding Underserved or Overlooked Markets

- **"How did Francis Greenburger find an entire market hiding in plain sight?"** A masterclass in [Francis Greenburger](https://www.foundersnotes.com/transcripts/2033/?location=00:30:11)—discovering an unmet need by being his own customer, then scaling relentlessly: "Discovering an unmet need in the market is a tremendous start to any business."

- **"How did people go 'to where no one else is' to set their own prices?"** This connects Sam Zell's thesis in [Am I Being Too Subtle?](https://www.foundersnotes.com/bookreview/1360/?highlight=44033)—building a portfolio in smaller, high-growth markets nobody else was serving—with the broader pattern of avoiding competition.

- **"What did Black Titan teach about figuring out what a community actually needs?"** This surfaces the pivotal mental shift in [Black Titan](https://www.foundersnotes.com/bookreview/1581/?highlight=55189)—from "what can I sell?" to "what does this community actually need?"

## Prompts on Getting the Big Trend Right

- **"Why does the author of How to Make a Few Billion Dollars say getting the major trend right covers a multitude of sins?"** This unlocks one of the best market lessons in the collection from [How to Make a Few Billion Dollars](https://www.foundersnotes.com/bookreview/1351/?highlight=43489): "You can mess up a lot of things in business and still do well as long as you get the big trend right"—paired with his obsessive industry research.

- **"How did Tom Murphy and Ted Turner see cable TV as an opportunity instead of a threat?"** A great case study in reading trends correctly from [Tom Murphy](https://www.foundersnotes.com/transcripts/2055/?location=00:18:03)—while every other broadcaster panicked, they recognized "this is not a threat, it's an opportunity."

- **"How did Larry Ellison spot the database market opportunity that no one else acted on?"** This surfaces a key lesson in [Larry Ellison (Oracle)](https://www.foundersnotes.com/transcripts/2277/?location=00:33:59)—lots of people read the same IBM paper, but "only Ellison saw the opportunity to build an actual database product."

## Prompts on Choosing Markets with Great Economics

- **"What does Charlie Munger say is the difference between a good business and a bad business?"** This pulls his memorable framing in [Tao of Charlie Munger](https://www.foundersnotes.com/bookreview/1642/?highlight=62279): "good businesses throw up one easy decision after another. The bad businesses throw up painful decisions time after time."

- **"What makes the best kind of business according to James Dyson?"** This surfaces his philosophy in [Against the Odds](https://www.foundersnotes.com/bookreview/1558/?highlight=53983)—high price, good margin, enormous volume—and why this "high-risk" path is actually safer than following the herd.

- **"What's the difference between a market-size unconstrained opportunity and a normal one?"** This unlocks Jeff Bezos' electric realization in [Invent and Wander](https://www.foundersnotes.com/bookreview/1575/?highlight=88796)—being "doubly blessed" with both an unconstrained market and improving foundational technology: "That is not normal."

## Prompts on Knowing Your Market Deeply

- **"Why does the founder of Estée Lauder say all marketing insight comes from talking to customers?"** This taps the wisdom in [Mind Your Own Business](https://www.foundersnotes.com/bookreview/1427/?highlight=48320)—and the essential question: "Know what business you are really in."

- **"How do I know if I've truly found product-market fit?"** This surfaces Andy Rachleff's sharp test quoted in [The Tao of Warren Buffett](https://www.foundersnotes.com/transcripts/2130/?location=00:23:55): "The only way to know that you have product market fit is if you have word-of-mouth growth."
