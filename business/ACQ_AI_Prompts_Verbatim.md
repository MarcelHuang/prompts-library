## #1 SALES SCRIPT ANALYSIS

I will paste 10 full transcripts of successful sales calls that sold the same offer to the same type of prospect.

Your job is to build ONE "mother script" from these calls by extracting only what actually moves the sale forward and deleting everything else.

Follow these rules:

### 1. Analyze the calls first
1. Identify the common structure the best calls follow (from greeting to close).
2. Note the exact questions and phrases that consistently lead to opens, disclosure, commitment, and payment.

### 2. Define the call structure
Build the script in clear sections, with headings:
1. Opener & Rapport
2. Frame / Agenda
3. Discovery (problem, impact, budget, decision process, timing)
4. Transition to Pitch
5. Offer & Value Stack
6. Handling Top 3–5 Objections
7. Final Close & Next Steps (card in hand, booking, or BAMFAM)

### 3. Create the unified script
1. Use only lines that appear in multiple winning calls or are clearly essential glue.
2. Remove filler, small talk, and stories that don't directly help the sale.
3. Keep language simple, direct, and conversational (3rd–6th grade reading level).
4. Keep the core pitch portion short enough to be said in under 5 minutes at a normal pace.
5. Where useful, include [QUESTIONS] the rep should ask verbatim and [NOTES] about intent or tone.

### 4. Output format
1. A short overview of the structure in bullet points.
2. The final word‑for‑word script, organized by section headings.
3. A bullet list of the top 10 most powerful phrases/lines you pulled from the calls.

Remember: prioritize clarity, brevity, and closing power. Delete everything that doesn't help the prospect decide and buy.

## #4 UNIVERSAL WRITING STYLE ANALYZER

### PROMPT

I will paste a sample of writing after this prompt.
Analyze it deeply and systematically.

### 1. HIGH-LEVEL SNAPSHOT

Give a quick overview first:

- **Overall Style Summary (3–5 sentences):**
  Describe the "feel" of the writing as if you were explaining it to another writer who's never seen it.
- **Author Persona (inferred):**
  What kind of person does this voice sound like? (e.g., mentor, friend, drill sergeant, professor, entertainer, etc.)
- **Primary Goals of the Writing:**
  (e.g., persuade, teach, entertain, inspire, provoke, clarify, sell, etc.)

### 2. VOICE & TONE

Break down:
- **Voice:**
  - Is it formal/informal?
  - Direct or indirect?
  - Conversational, authoritative, playful, blunt, etc.?
- **Tone Shifts:**
  - Does the tone stay consistent or change?
  - Any notable moments where the tone becomes more intense, emotional, humorous, or serious?
- **Relationship to the Reader:**
  - Does the writer speak as a peer, expert, coach, storyteller, teacher, or something else?
  - Is the voice empathetic, challenging, supportive, confrontational?

### 3. STRUCTURE & FLOW

Analyze how the text is built:
- **Overall Structure Pattern:**
  - Common patterns (e.g., hook → story → lesson → framework → CTA).
  - Are there distinct sections or acts?
- **Openings:**
  - How does the writer typically start? (Bold claim, question, story, pattern interrupt, data, analogy, etc.)
- **Closings:**
  - How does the writer end? (Summary, punchline, call to action, reflection, challenge to reader, etc.)
- **Logical Flow:**
  - Is the argument/idea progression linear, looping, tangential, modular?
  - Frequent use of transitions? Which kinds? ("So…", "Here's why…", "But there's a catch…")

### 4. SENTENCE-LEVEL STYLE

Drill into how sentences are written:
- **Sentence Length & Rhythm:**
  - Mostly short? Mostly long? Mixed?
  - Does the writer use short punchy lines for emphasis?
- **Syntax & Construction:**
  - Simple vs. complex sentences.
  - Any patterns like: "stacked clauses," "lists," "if/then," "because" chains.
- **Punctuation Habits:**
  - Use of dashes, ellipses, parentheses, colons, semicolons, question marks, exclamation points.
  - Any stylistic quirks?
- **Use of Questions:**
  - How often do they ask questions?
  - Are they rhetorical, guiding, challenging?

### 5. DICTION & WORD CHOICE

Detail what kind of words they use:
- **Vocabulary Level:**
  - Simple, plain language vs. advanced/technical vocabulary.
- **Jargon & Domain Language:**
  - Do they use industry terms? If so, how heavily?
- **Repetition & Catchphrases:**
  - Any phrases, words, or patterns the writer leans on frequently?
- **Metaphors, Analogies, Imagery:**
  - How often? What kind? (e.g., sports, war, games, physics, relationships, etc.)

### 6. RHETORICAL & PERSUASIVE DEVICES

Call out the mechanics of persuasion:
- **Storytelling Usage:**
  - Personal anecdotes, case studies, hypothetical stories, parables?
- **Framing & Reframing:**
  - How does the writer flip conventional beliefs or reframe problems?
- **Use of Contrast:**
  - Before/after, old way/new way, them/us, pain/pleasure.
- **Authority Signals:**
  - Data, credentials, experience, social proof, contrarian takes.
- **Emotion Triggers:**
  - Guilt, hope, status, fear, curiosity, relief, belonging, ambition?

### 7. FORMATTING & PRESENTATION

Look at how it looks on the page/screen:
- **Paragraph Length:**
  - Many short paragraphs, or long blocks of text?
- **Line Breaks & White Space:**
  - Do they isolate single sentences for emphasis?
- **Use of Lists/Bullets/Numbering:**
  - How often? For what purpose?
- **Headings & Subheadings:**
  - Are there clear headers? How are they phrased? (Curious, direct, benefit-driven?)

### 8. STYLE RULES / CHECKLIST

Turn all of the above into a practical checklist a writer could follow to imitate this style.
Format this as "If you want to write in this style, do X":
- 5–10 "Voice Rules"
- 5–10 "Sentence & Word Choice Rules"
- 5–10 "Structure & Flow Rules"
- 3–5 "Do NOT" rules (things that would obviously break this style)

Example format (customized to this text, not generic):
- "Use short, punchy sentences to land important points."
- "Start with a bold, slightly contrarian statement that challenges the reader's default belief."
- "Avoid academic language and qualifiers like 'perhaps' or 'arguably.'"

### 9. MINI EMULATION SAMPLE (OPTIONAL)

Using your analysis, write a short paragraph (5–8 sentences) about the topic:
"Why most people fail to execute on good ideas"

Write it in the same style as the analyzed text.
This is just to show the style in action.

### 10. OUTPUT FORMAT

Respond in this structure:
1. High-Level Snapshot
2. Voice & Tone
3. Structure & Flow
4. Sentence-Level Style
5. Diction & Word Choice
6. Rhetorical & Persuasive Devices
7. Formatting & Presentation
8. Style Rules / Checklist
9. Mini Emulation Sample

Now I will paste the writing sample below. Do not start analyzing until after the sample.
When you respond, be specific and concrete—no vague, generic advice.
