### 📊 Executive Summary Deck
**Format:** Detailed deck
**Length:** Short

> Distill the key insights from this material into a tight executive briefing deck. Lead each slide with a single bold assertion (the "so what"), followed by supporting evidence. Use a clean, corporate aesthetic — dark navy and white palette, minimal decoration. Every slide should answer: why does this matter?

---

### 🗣️ Presenter Slides — TED-Style
**Format:** Presenter slides
**Length:** Default

> Create minimal presenter slides in the style of a TED talk. Each slide should have one central idea, one striking visual or diagram, and no more than 10 words of text. The flow should build tension and release — start with a provocative question, develop the argument, and close with a memorable takeaway.

---

### 📖 Story Arc Deck
**Format:** Presenter slides
**Length:** Default

> Transform this content into a narrative slide deck structured like a story: opening hook, conflict/problem, rising complexity, resolution, and call to action. Use the "What / So What / Now What" framework for each major section. Keep the visual language consistent — icons, simple diagrams, and bold section headers.

---

### 🧠 Concept Map Deck
**Format:** Detailed deck
**Length:** Default

> Convert this material into a slide deck centered on concept maps. Each major concept gets its own slide showing how it connects to sub-concepts using arrows, hierarchies, and relationship labels. Use color-coding to distinguish between categories. Aim to make the relationships between ideas the star of the show, not just the definitions.

---

### 🔁 Before & After Deck
**Format:** Presenter slides
**Length:** Short

> Structure this content as a before-and-after transformation deck. Each section should contrast the old way vs. the new way using side-by-side layouts. Highlight what changed, why it matters, and what the outcome looks like. Use a consistent visual metaphor throughout (e.g., rough sketch → polished diagram).

## 💡 Tips for Better Results

- **Be specific about the audience** — "for a first-year university student" vs. "for a senior analyst" produces very different outputs.
- **Name the aesthetic** — referencing a style (e.g., "Economist magazine", "MIT OpenCourseWare", "hand-drawn sketchnote") anchors the visual direction quickly.
- **For slide decks**, "Presenter slides" + a strong description usually outperforms "Detailed deck" if you plan to speak to them live.
- **For infographics**, "Landscape + Detailed" works best for complex topics; "Portrait + Concise" for quick-reference material.
- **Combine prompts** — e.g., use Professor's Whiteboard orientation with the Data Story description for a visual lecture summary of stats-heavy content.
